How to use the device:

Disclaimer: Users on iOS or mobile browsers other than Chrome will not be able to connect to the device, but can still use the text input and pitch accent display features.

To connect to the device, click the "Connect to device" button on the scanner page after turning on your bluetooth and select "Yubine-Device" from the list of Bluetooth devices (make sure it is turned on and loaded fully). Once connected, you can scan Japanese text using the camera or input it manually. The device will then display the pitch accent pattern for the scanned word, allowing you to feel it through the tactile system.

** It is highly recommended to use Yomitan with some good dictionaries alongside Yubine.

SRSF Project Info:

Learning vocabulary in a second is a tedious process, and for Japanese it's especially challenging due to each word having a pitch accent that can be tricky for learners to perceive. Existing tools focus on dictionary lookups or visual/audio cues to aid learners during language immersion. Studies have shown a correlation between touch and increased retention during the acquisition of new vocabulary, but no widely used tool aimed at streamlining immersion using this has been created. We developed a tool that maps Japanese pitch accent onto a tactile system that allows learners to feel different pitch patterns with their hands. It does this by moving mechanical cams using stepper motors representing pitch through high/low pegs. We have an accompanying website where you can scan the desired word. Preliminary testing suggests that using the device could improve retention. This project demonstrates a novel approach to multisensory language learning.

What are Japanese pitch accents?

Japanese has high/low accents for its sounds. The pitch of a word can be mapped across each character in the word, with each respective character in a word having either a high or low pitch. For example, the Japanese word kazoku, ka’s pitch is high, zo’s pitch is low, and ku’s pitch is low. Japanese also has “particles”, which are grammar markers, which have differing pitches depending on the word. For kazoku, its particle’s pitch is low. Kazoku’s pitch pattern can be represented by a string of H and Ls being HLL(L) or a graph. There are four classifications of pitch patterns, heiban, oudaka, nakadaka, and atamadaka. Pitch patterns are like grammar rules where there are general rules but trying to memorize every single rule has diminishing returns.
